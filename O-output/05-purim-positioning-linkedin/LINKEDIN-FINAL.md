# LinkedIn Post — FINAL

Haman had a positioning problem.

He thought he understood the power dynamics. He had the king's ear. He had a plan. Everything was clear.

Then he realized he'd completely misread the situation.

This is the story of Purim, but it's also the story of founders who skip positioning and go straight to tactics.

---

**Here's what Haman got wrong:**

He saw a tactical opportunity (removing a specific person) and took it.

He didn't see the strategic vulnerability (he didn't know Esther was the one person who could influence the king).

He optimized for the wrong goal (revenge on Mordecai instead of understanding the dynamics).

Sound familiar?

---

**I see this constantly:**

A founder launches a product. They have a feature advantage. They know the market.

Then competitors pass them. Deals stall. Messaging gets confused.

Why? Same reason Haman miscalculated.

They optimized for the wrong thing. They saw the feature (the tactic). They missed the positioning (the dynamic).

---

**What Esther and Mordecai did differently:**

They didn't have the advantage. They didn't have the plan. They had incomplete information.

But they understood one thing: **the truth always matters more than the narrative.**

When Esther revealed herself — when she made the hidden visible — the entire dynamic shifted.

Because clarity beats tactics. Strategy beats speed.

---

**The Purim parallel for B2B founders:**

Your competitive advantage isn't your feature. It's your clarity about what you actually solve and for whom.

Haman lost because he thought he understood the game but didn't.

Founders lose the same way — when they think they understand positioning but it's actually broken.

---

**Tomorrow on Purim, the message is simple:**

Revelation beats concealment.

Clarity beats confusion.

Strategy beats tactics.

And the person who understands the *actual* dynamic always beats the person who just has a good plan.

What's your Purim positioning moment — the thing you've been avoiding revealing?
